This paper analyzes the economic feasibility of producing perovskite solar cells using roll-to-roll methods augmented by Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) annealing processes. The authors explore how such a production method could scale up sufficiently to meet global photovoltaic demand.

A techno-economic model is developed, highlighting cost implications stemming from materials, labor, equipment, and energy requirements for large-scale output. One key finding is that the use of IPL can reduce costs associated with facility footprint, operation, and equipment when compared to traditional thermal annealing techniques.

The study provides a potential pathway towards affordable perovskite solar cells by demonstrating economically viable production rates and cost structures. The analysis predicts per solar film production costs between $0.04 and $0.10 per watt when operating at gigawatt-scale levels. Such advancements underscore the economic viability of perovskite technology as an alternative to conventional silicon in large-scale applications.

Material costs, particularly materials, are identified as dominant factors driving manufacturing expenditure. In contrast, the IPL annealing process is recommended for its benefits over conventional thermal ovens, including reduced heat transfer issues and operational expenditures, thus positioning it favorably within roll-to-roll production strategies.
